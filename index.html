Maths Education in Ireland

“The quality of an education system cannot exceed the quality of its teachers.”

“The only way to improve outcomes is to improve instruction.”

https://mckinsey.com/industries/social-sector/our-insights/how-the-worlds-best-performing-school-systems-come-out-on-top

In a response to the proposal for bonus points in maths, committees in the Royal Irish Academy wrote: “Teacher qualifications are key to the overall quality of the teaching experience. The availability of teachers whose primary degree qualification is in Mathematics significantly determines the overall quality of the teaching and learning experience for students of LC Mathematics.”

https://www.ria.ie/sites/default/files/submission-on-bonus-points-for-maths-29-feb-2008-1.pdf

In the 1990s, not enough maths graduates were going into teaching so the NUI universities created an easier maths degree programme ‘Mathematical Studies’.

https://www.ucc.ie/en/matsci/outreach/prospective-teachers/

Say the points for a certain NUI Professional Masters in Education (secondary school teacher training course) were 45, a ‘Mathematical Studies’ graduate with 66% would get a place while an Honours Mathematics graduate with 65% would not. The B.A. in Mathematics and Education which was also designed for prospective secondary school maths teachers only exists in NUIG. This course which is of a higher standard than ‘Mathematical Studies’ could have been rolled out to other universities. In order to attract enough people into maths teaching, we have a situation where someone who wants to be a maths teacher will find it easier if they do not try to get a maths degree.

https://pac.ie/pmeinfo/PointsCalc.php

https://nuigalway.ie/courses/undergraduate-courses/arts-mathematics-and-education.html

But it is not enough, the Department of Education and Skills funds an upskilling programme for teachers unqualified to teach maths (Professional Diploma in Mathematics for Teaching).

https://www.gov.ie/en/publication/d5661-new-post-primary-upskilling-programmes/

The decline in the standard of maths that teachers have has led to a decline in the standard of maths that teachers teach.

‘Project Maths’ is the codename for the secondary school maths syllabus – a reduced syllabus – developed in an irregular way by the NCCA (National Council for Curriculum and Assessment). The syllabus is vague and the marking scheme is “very generous”. They never conducted Randomised Control Trials, even though this could have been easily done using the pilot schools.

https://maths.ucd.ie/docserve?id=60

https://irishtimes.com/sponsored/exam-times/maths/project-maths-a-teacher-s-perspective-1.1663014

‘Research into the impact of Project Maths on student achievement, learning and motivation’ did compare pilot and non-pilot schools but only examined parts of the course where students had studied the new course, so they were really only looking at how well the new course was being implemented. A more accurate title would be ‘Research into the impact of having teachers who were more familiar with the course on student achievement, learning and motivation’.

https://ncca.ie/media/2643/impact_of_project_maths_final_nfer_2013.pdf

“When teaching new content and skills to novices, teachers are more effective when they provide explicit guidance accompanied by practice and feedback, not when they require students to discover many aspects of what they must learn.”

“Small group and independent problems and projects can be effective - not as vehicles for making discoveries, but as a means of practicing recently learned content and skills.”

https://aft.org/sites/default/files/periodicals/Clark.pdf

Countries that perform well in international tests cater properly for students who want to study advanced mathematics to prepare them for STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics) subjects at third level.

Students who want to study STEM subjects in the future know that maths is something they will need. There is no need to under-prepare these students in order to engage everyone else with a veneer of “relevancy”.

“Teach for understanding” (‘teach less maths’) is a quality versus quantity argument. Is the quality and quantity lower in Ireland?

A report commissioned by the NCCA, Leaving Certificate Mathematics: a comparative analysis compared ‘Project Maths’ with five other countries/jurisdictions. The NCCA interpreted the report as ‘Project Maths’ being broadly in line with the five other countries/jurisdictions. However, a more accurate interpretation would be that ‘Project Maths’ prepared students the least for third level.

The report concluded that the NCCA may wish to consider should vectors, matrices and differential equations be included in ‘Project Maths’. “Obviously any additional content may be at the expense of synthetic geometry, proof and the emphasis on statistics, some distinctive elements that Ireland may consider worth retaining.” It is crazy that these are emphasised at the expense of areas more relevant to other STEM subjects.

https://ncca.ie/media/2121/lc-mathematics-comparative-analysis.pdf

The report of the STEM Education Review Group stated:

“Although the % of students taking Higher Level Mathematics at Leaving Certificate has increased significantly (from 16% in 2011 to 28% in 2016) since the introduction of Bonus CAO Points, there are serious concerns about the ‘mathematical under-preparedness’ of students entering third level and about the lack of basic skills of some students sitting the Higher Level paper.”

https://assets.gov.ie/25068/d5c86a91ac3b43869f827438f58d88c0.pdf

PISA = Programme for International Student Assessment

TIMSS = Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study

The top 5 countries/economies for ‘mathematical literacy’ in PISA 2018 and 4th grade and 8th grade mathematics in TIMSS 2019 are in Eastern Asia, all of which have courses catering for students who want to study STEM at third level.

TIMSS scores for Ireland in 8th grade (2nd Year) maths

Year 1995 2015 2019
Score 527 523 524

Ireland’s TIMSS 2019 2nd Year maths scores in Algebra (505) and Geometry (506) are not much higher than the median (500). Most of 1st Year is spent revising primary school maths. Putting off learning challenging topics denies students the time to consolidate their learning. While ensuring that students who come from primary schools that are not strong in maths are not at a disadvantage in secondary school is laudable, the Department of Education and Skills could provide a course in maths for primary school teachers (like the upskilling course for secondary school teachers).

‘TIMSS Advanced’ measures preparedness for the study of STEM subjects at third level, but Ireland does not participate in it.

https://timss2015.org/advanced

‘Project Maths’ has a greater emphasis on PISA-like problems, yet students’ ability to solve such problems in a context has not improved (see PISA scores). This greater emphasis has meant a lesser emphasis on preparing students to study STEM at third level, even though this problem could be resolved by introducing an additional subject: Further Maths.

https://erc.ie/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/PM_EvaluationStrand1.pdf

Despite these improvements secondary school students of 50 years ago would be ready for any university course today. For example, Particle Physics is only a tiny part of the Physics curriculum, everything else they would know. The most modern topic on the maths curriculum 50 years ago was Linear Algebra (vectors and matrices).

Students will go on to work with (and compete against) people from other countries, not so much people from previous generations. Are students now being well prepared for the future?

University education departments would not exist if they did not train secondary school teachers. A more precise name would be ‘secondary school teacher training’ departments. Nearly all trainee teachers are university graduates when they start their teacher training (or are studying towards a university concurrent degree). There is no longer any reason for training secondary school teachers away from a secondary school in a university (a centralised location with large lecture theatres). ‘Teaching schools’ would be a more appropriate place to train teachers, being trained by experienced teachers instead of pseudo-academics. University education departments award a “PhD in Education” to people who have spent years researching secondary school teaching (“passive learning”), most of whom it seems left teaching after a few years, who go on to lecture trainee teachers about “active learning”. Teacher training by experienced and current teachers in a cluster of nearby schools certified by the State Examinations Commission would be preferable to teacher training by former teachers in universities most of whom (it seems) do not have a credible amount of teaching experience and mostly do not conduct credible research like Randomised Control Trials.

How many years were “education” (teaching) lecturers teaching?, and why did they leave it?

Career progression for experienced teachers to become a teacher trainer would enhance the teaching profession and would mean teacher trainers would have a credible amount of teaching experience.

https://openknowledge.worldbank.org/bitstream/handle/10986/24000/9781464807909.pdf#page=59

Having such a system where teachers are incentivised to teach in more challenging schools because they can progress in their career faster would do a lot more good than all the central planning of false start teachers who are now educationalists.
