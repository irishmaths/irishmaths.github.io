Maths Education in Ireland

“The quality of an education system cannot exceed the quality of its teachers.”

“The only way to improve outcomes is to improve instruction.”

https://mckinsey.com/industries/social-sector/our-insights/how-the-worlds-best-performing-school-systems-come-out-on-top

In a response to the proposal for bonus points in maths, committees in the Royal Irish Academy wrote: “Teacher qualifications are key to the overall quality of the teaching experience. The availability of teachers whose primary degree qualification is in Mathematics significantly determines the overall quality of the teaching and learning experience for students of LC Mathematics.”

https://ria.ie/assets/uploads/2024/06/submission-on-bonus-points-for-maths-29-feb-2008-1.pdf

In the 1990s, not enough maths graduates were going into teaching so the NUI universities created an easier maths degree programme ‘Mathematical Studies’.

https://ucc.ie/en/matsci/outreach/prospective-teachers/

The B.A. in Mathematics and Education which was also designed for prospective secondary school maths teachers only exists in University of Galway. This course which is of a higher standard than ‘Mathematical Studies’ could have been rolled out to other universities.

https://universityofgalway.ie/courses/undergraduate-courses/arts-mathematics-and-education.html

The Department of Education funds an upskilling programme for teachers unqualified to teach maths (Professional Diploma in Mathematics for Teaching).

https://gov.ie/en/publication/d5661-new-post-primary-upskilling-programmes/

The decline in the standard of maths that teachers have has led to a decline in the standard of maths that teachers teach.

‘Project Maths’ is the codename for the secondary school maths syllabus – a reduced syllabus – developed in an irregular way by the NCCA (National Council for Curriculum and Assessment). The syllabus marking scheme is “very generous”.

https://maths.ucd.ie/docserve?id=60

https://irishtimes.com/sponsored/exam-times/maths/project-maths-a-teacher-s-perspective-1.1663014

Countries that perform well in international tests cater properly for students who want to study advanced mathematics to prepare them for STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, Mathematics) subjects at third level.

Students who want to study STEM subjects in the future know that maths is something they will need. There is no need to under-prepare these students in order to engage everyone else with a veneer of “relevancy”.

“Teach for understanding” (‘teach less maths’) is a quality versus quantity argument. The quality is dependent on the teachers, which in turn will influence the quantity.

A report commissioned by the NCCA, ‘Leaving Certificate Mathematics: a comparative analysis’ compared ‘Project Maths’ with five other countries/jurisdictions. It concluded that the NCCA may wish to consider should vectors, matrices and differential equations be included in ‘Project Maths’. “Obviously any additional content may be at the expense of synthetic geometry, proof and the emphasis on statistics, some distinctive elements that Ireland may consider worth retaining.” It is crazy that these are emphasised at the expense of areas more relevant to other STEM subjects.

https://ncca.ie/media/2121/lc-mathematics-comparative-analysis.pdf

The report of the STEM Education Review Group stated:

“Although the % of students taking Higher Level Mathematics at Leaving Certificate has increased significantly (from 16% in 2011 to 28% in 2016) since the introduction of Bonus CAO Points, there are serious concerns about the ‘mathematical under-preparedness’ of students entering third level and about the lack of basic skills of some students sitting the Higher Level paper.”

https://assets.gov.ie/25068/d5c86a91ac3b43869f827438f58d88c0.pdf

PISA = Programme for International Student Assessment

TIMSS = Trends in International Mathematics and Science Study

The top 5 countries/economies for ‘mathematical literacy’ in PISA 2022 and 4th class and 2nd Year mathematics in TIMSS 2023 are in Eastern Asia, all of which have courses catering for students who want to study STEM at third level.

TIMSS scores for Ireland in 2nd Year maths

Year 1995 2015 2019 2023
Score 527 523 524 522

Ireland’s TIMSS 2023 2nd Year maths score in Algebra was 19 points lower than the overall score (no other country had a greater difference). Most of 1st Year is spent revising primary school maths. Putting off learning challenging topics denies students the time to consolidate their learning. The Department of Education could provide a course in maths for primary school teachers (like the upskilling course for secondary school teachers).

https://timss2023.org/results/grade-8-math-content/

‘Project Maths’ has a greater emphasis on PISA-like problems, yet students’ ability to solve such problems in a context has not improved (see PISA scores). This greater emphasis has meant a lesser emphasis on preparing students to study STEM at third level, even though this problem could be resolved by introducing an additional subject: Further Maths. Since UK students study fewer subjects than Irish students, Further Maths is more needed in Ireland than in the UK.

https://erc.ie/wp-content/uploads/2017/11/PM_EvaluationStrand1.pdf

In PISA 2022, 7% of students in Ireland were top performers in maths (OECD average: 9%). The Department of Education claim the solution to this is a “greater use of open-ended, enquiry-based learning”. Ideas that educationalists have been promoting for decades are not going to improve the situation. Randomised Control Trials could show how ineffective enquiry-based learning would be compared to a more rigorous curriculum.

Ironically, secondary school students of 50 years ago would be better prepared than students today for any STEM university course. The most modern topic on the maths curriculum 50 years ago was Linear Algebra (vectors and matrices). Modern ‘Data Science’ (computer science, statistics, and machine learning) relies on advanced Calculus and Linear Algebra. Nowadays, students know more Statistics, however individual topics in Statistics can be learned with little prior knowledge.

TIMSS and PISA assess 2nd and 3rd Year students. ‘TIMSS Advanced’ assesses advanced mathematics final year students, but has only run once a decade. Ireland has never participated in it.

https://timss2015.org/advanced

University education departments would not exist if they did not train secondary school teachers. A more precise name would be ‘secondary school teacher training’ departments. Nearly all trainee teachers are university graduates when they start their teacher training (or are studying towards a university concurrent degree). There is no longer any reason for training secondary school teachers away from a secondary school in a university (a centralised location with large lecture theatres). ‘Teaching schools’ would be a more appropriate place to train teachers, being trained by experienced teachers instead of pseudo-academics. University education departments award a “PhD in Education” to people who have spent years researching secondary school teaching (“passive learning”), most of whom it seems left teaching after a few years, who go on to lecture trainee teachers about “active learning”. Teacher training by experienced and current teachers in a cluster of nearby schools certified by the State Examinations Commission would be preferable to teacher training by former teachers in universities most of whom (it seems) do not have a credible amount of teaching experience.

Career progression for experienced teachers to become a teacher trainer would make the teaching profession more attractive. This is what they do in Shanghai, whereas in Ireland teacher trainers could not leave teaching fast enough.
